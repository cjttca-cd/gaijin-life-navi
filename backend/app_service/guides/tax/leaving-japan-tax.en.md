# Tax Settlement Guide Before Leaving Japan

When returning home or leaving Japan for an extended period, many foreigners assume that once they've completed their resignation and apartment checkout procedures, they're all done. But tax obligations don't simply vanish when you cross the border — if you don't handle things in advance, you could end up receiving tax notices after you've left, accumulating late fees, or facing even more serious consequences. Here are the key tax matters to confirm before you depart.

---

## 住民税: The Myth That "Leaving Means You Don't Have to Pay"

住民税 is calculated based on **the previous year's income**, not the current year's. The 住民税 notice that arrives around June each year corresponds to income earned the year before.

Even if you've already left Japan during the current year, as long as you were **residing in Japan on January 1**, you are still required to pay the full amount of 住民税 for that year — you cannot be exempted simply because you've left the country. Even if you're already back home when the June notice arrives, the payment obligation remains. This is a duty that many people don't realize before they leave.

## 納税管理人 (Tax Representative) — An Important Step Before You Leave

If you still have tax obligations after leaving Japan (such as receiving a tax refund, paying taxes, or receiving tax notices), you'll need to designate a **納税管理人** with the 税務署 and your local 役所 before you depart.

### Who Can Be a 納税管理人?

- No need to be a 税理士 — **a family member, friend, or colleague** can serve in this role
- The person must be a permanent resident of Japan

### Things to Note When Designating One

- Depending on the municipality, you may need to **submit separate designation forms to the 税務署 and the 役所** — confirm with each office in advance
- If your 納税管理人 also leaves Japan later, it will break the line of communication and late fees will continue to accumulate — make sure to confirm their long-term plans to remain in Japan

### Connection to the Pension Refund

Applying for 脱退一時金 (pension lump-sum withdrawal payment) and reclaiming the 20.42% withholding tax may also require your 納税管理人 to file a 確定申告. Plan your pension paperwork alongside this process.

## How to Pay 住民税 After Leaving Japan

Payment options are limited once you've left Japan, so make arrangements in advance:

| Payment Method | Notes |
|----------------|-------|
| **口座振替 (Auto-debit)** | Set this up at your bank before leaving, and make sure sufficient funds remain in your account. The most reliable option. |
| Credit card | Supported by some municipalities, though usually with an additional handling fee |
| コンビニ / PayPay etc. | Only usable within Japan — **cannot be used from overseas** |

## 国外財産調書 — Required if You Hold Large Overseas Assets

If you hold **overseas assets exceeding ¥50 million** as of **December 31** each year, you are required to submit a 「国外財産調書」 to the 税務署.

Failing to file, or filing with errors, can result in additional **加算税** (penalty surcharges). This is an obligation that many foreign residents in Japan aren't aware of — please review based on your own asset situation.

---

## Extra Considerations for Property Owners

If you plan to continue renting out Japanese real estate after leaving:

- You'll generally need to arrange both a **property management company** and a **納税管理人** — you need both
- Tenants are required to apply **源泉徴収 (withholding tax at 20.42%)** to rental payments, but many tenants are unaware of this obligation, which can lead to disputes
- It's worth calculating "rental income − expenses − amount after 源泉徴収" before deciding whether to rent out the property
