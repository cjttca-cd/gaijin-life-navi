# Tax Basics for Foreigners Living in Japan

If you're living in Japan long-term, taxes are something you simply can't ignore. Regardless of nationality, anyone with a registered address in Japan is required to file and pay taxes under Japanese law. Many newcomers aren't familiar with the Japanese tax system — and often get hit with a surprisingly large bill in their second year. Getting a handle on the basics early can help you avoid late-payment penalties and keep your visa renewal or permanent residency application on track.

---

## The Two Main Taxes You Need to Know

Foreign residents in Japan typically deal with two main types of tax:

**① 所得税 (National Income Tax)**  
Collected by the national government (国税庁), this tax applies to your total annual income using a progressive rate structure. If you work for a Japanese company, your employer withholds tax from your salary every month — this is called 「源泉徴収」. At year-end, your employer performs 「年末調整」, a final reconciliation of the tax withheld throughout the year (more on this below).

**② 住民税 (Resident Tax)**  
Collected by your local municipality (役所). The key thing to understand: **住民税 is calculated based on the previous year's income and starts being charged from June of the following year**. This timing catches a lot of foreign residents off guard —

---

## ⚠️ No 住民税 in Your First Year — But It Hits in Year Two

In your first year in Japan, there's no record of Japanese income from the prior year, so you typically won't owe 住民税. But **starting in your second year**, a 住民税 bill will appear — and the amount can be quite a shock. It's a good idea to set some money aside before year two arrives.

## Employees: 年末調整 Takes Care of Most of It

If you work for a Japanese company, your employer handles 「年末調整」 each December — comparing the tax withheld throughout the year against what you actually owe, then issuing a refund or collecting the difference. **If you have a single employer and no other income sources, you generally don't need to file anything extra after 年末調整.**

---

## When Do You Need to File Yourself (確定申告)?

The following situations typically require you to submit 「確定申告」 to your local tax office between **February 16 and March 15** of the following year:

- Self-employed individuals and freelancers
- People receiving wages from two or more employers
- Side income exceeding a certain threshold
- Foreign-source income (e.g., interest on overseas bank accounts)
- Claiming specific deductions such as medical expense deductions

> **Important:** Even if your side income is under ¥200,000 and you don't need to file 所得税, **you may still need to file a 住民税 return separately with your local municipality**. "No 所得税 filing needed" does not mean "no 住民税 filing needed." Always check with your 役所.

## 住民税 and Your Visa: Tax Records Matter

Your 住民税 payment history directly affects visa renewals and permanent residency applications. Authorities typically ask for a 「**納税証明書**」 — note that this is a completely different document from a 「課税証明書」. When requesting one from your 役所, make sure you're asking for the right type. Keep all your tax payment receipts safe.

## Key Dates to Remember

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| Every June | 住民税 billing begins (employees usually have it deducted by their employer) |
| February 16 (following year) | 確定申告 filing period opens |
| March 15 (following year) | 確定申告 filing deadline (standard) |
