# Japan's 住民税 Guide — What Every Foreign Resident Needs to Know

Living in Japan means paying more than just national income tax. You're also responsible for **住民税** (じゅうみんぜい) — a local tax collected by the municipality where you live. The money goes toward local services like schools, waste management, and road maintenance. Many foreign residents are caught off guard when the bill arrives, often wondering why the amount jumped so suddenly. This guide breaks down the basics so you know what to expect and can avoid common pitfalls.

---

## How 住民税 Works

住民税 is calculated based on **the previous year's income**, with January 1st as the reference date each year.

- If you didn't have a 住民登録 in Japan as of January 1st, you typically **don't owe 住民税 for that year**
- Starting from the second year, you'll be billed based on your income from the first year

This time lag is exactly why so many foreign residents experience the "second-year shock" (see below).

---

## Payment Methods: 特別徴収 vs 普通徴収

| Method | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **特別徴収** | Your employer deducts the tax directly from your monthly salary. Common for company employees. The amount is recalculated every June. |
| **普通徴収** | The municipality sends a payment notice directly to you, usually in June. You pay in installments. Common for freelancers and self-employed individuals. |

If you don't see a 住民税 deduction on your payslip, you're likely on 普通徴収 — keep an eye on your mailbox around June.

## The "Second-Year Shock" — Why Did the Bill Suddenly Get So Big?

Many people are blindsided in their second year in Japan — or after changing jobs — by a surprisingly large bill. The reason is the prior-year billing system:

> **住民税 is based on last year's income, and the notice doesn't arrive until June of the following year.**

If you receive a large lump-sum 普通徴収 notice, you can visit your local municipal tax counter (役所の税務窓口) to discuss a payment installment plan — municipalities generally accommodate this.

## 住民税 and Your Visa / Permanent Residency Application

Your 住民税 payment record is closely tied to visa renewals and 永住 applications. Keep these points in mind:

1. **Pay on time**: Late or missed payments can negatively affect your visa review. In any year you're planning a renewal, make 住民税 a priority.

2. **Keep your receipts**: Hold onto your payment receipts (領収書) from 普通徴収 payments — you may need them as documentation later.

3. **Know the difference between certificates**: For 永住 applications through the Immigration Services Agency (入管), you'll typically need a **納税証明書** (tax payment certificate), not a 課税証明書 (taxation certificate). They're different documents — confirm which one you need at your local 役所 counter.

## Other Common Questions

**ふるさと納税 in Your First Year**

Even if you don't owe 住民税 in your first year in Japan, you can still participate in ふるさと納税. Your donation will be deducted from the following year's 住民税, so don't skip it just because you don't have a bill yet.

**Side Income and Keeping It Private**

If you file a 確定申告 reporting side income, you can select the "self-payment (普通徴収)" option on the form to prevent your side income from being reported to your employer via the 特別徴収 notice. That said, each municipality handles this differently, so results aren't guaranteed.

**Tax Exemption Eligibility**

If your income is low enough, you may qualify for 住民税 non-taxable status (非課税). People in this category may be eligible for additional benefits in certain public welfare and support programs.
