# 帰化 Application (Acquiring Japanese Citizenship) Guide

For long-term foreign residents in Japan, 帰化 (naturalization) is a major life decision tied to national identity and future planning. Unlike applying for 永住権 (permanent residency), 帰化 means formally becoming a Japanese citizen — a fundamental change in nationality. After years of living in Japan, many foreign residents face a key choice: keep their original nationality and obtain 永住権, or give it up and become a Japanese citizen. This guide gives a high-level overview of the key differences to help you start thinking through this decision.

## 帰化 vs. 永住権: The Core Differences

Long-term foreign residents in Japan generally have two main paths: applying for **永住権** or **帰化 (acquiring Japanese citizenship)**. Both allow you to live in Japan long-term, but they differ fundamentally in legal status, rights, obligations, and how nationality is handled.

**永住権 (Permanent Residency)**
- You keep your original nationality and continue living in Japan as a foreign national
- You receive an indefinite residence status — no need to renew your 在留期間 regularly
- You enjoy treatment similar to Japanese citizens in most everyday situations
- Some differences remain, such as voting rights and eligibility for certain public positions
- You still need a 在留カード, which must be renewed within its validity period (typically every 7 years)

**帰化 (Acquiring Japanese Citizenship)**
- You formally become a Japanese citizen with Japanese nationality
- Japan generally does not recognize dual nationality, so you typically must renounce your original citizenship
- You gain full civil rights, including the right to vote
- No 在留カード required — you live in Japan as a Japanese citizen
- You can apply for a Japanese passport, opening more options for international travel and employment

## Things to Consider Before Choosing 帰化

帰化 is a life-changing decision. Before applying, it's worth thinking carefully about the following:

**Impact on Your Original Nationality Rights**
Renouncing your original nationality can affect various rights — property ownership, inheritance, and residency rights in your home country. The impact varies by nationality, so it's worth checking with your home country's embassy or consulate in Japan beforehand.

**Impact on Your Family**
How nationality is handled for family members (spouses, children) varies by situation. If family members have different nationalities, each case may need to be considered separately.

**Impact on Your Career**
Acquiring Japanese citizenship opens up certain job opportunities that were previously available only to Japanese nationals. However, if your work is closely tied to your home country, renouncing that citizenship could have downsides too — it's worth weighing both sides.

**Complexity and Professional Help**
The 帰化 application process is relatively complex, requires extensive documentation, and involves a lengthy review period. Applicants typically go through specific government agencies, and preparing all the necessary materials can be time-consuming. It's strongly recommended to seek help from a **行政書士** experienced in 帰化 applications to improve your chances of success and avoid common pitfalls.

## The Relationship Between 帰化 and 永住権

帰化 and 永住権 aren't mutually exclusive choices. In fact, many foreign residents first apply for 永住権, then — after fully understanding and weighing their options — decide whether to pursue 帰化 further. If you're currently more focused on the requirements and things to watch out for with 永住権, check out the **Permanent Residency Guide (V4)** for details on eligibility conditions and common pitfalls.

There's no universally "correct" answer when choosing between 帰化 and 永住権 — it comes down to your nationality situation, family plans, career goals, and how settled you feel in Japan for the long term. Take time to gather information and talk to professionals before making this important decision.
