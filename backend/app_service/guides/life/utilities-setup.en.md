# Setting Up Utilities in Japan

When you move into a new home in Japan, electricity, gas, and water (known collectively as「ライフライン」— "lifelines") **must each be set up separately by contacting each provider yourself**. This is different from some countries where the building management handles everything centrally. If you don't arrange this in advance, you may have no power or hot water on moving day — so be sure to sort it out before you move in.

---

## Overview of the Three ライフライン

### Electricity（電気）

Contact the electric company serving your area to set up service. You can usually do this through their **official website or by phone**. Once the application is complete, power will be activated. Japan's electricity market is partially liberalized, meaning you can choose from regional power companies or alternative suppliers (新電力), but all require going through official channels to start service.

Applying online is convenient — you can do it anytime without waiting on hold, which is especially helpful if your Japanese isn't strong. Make sure to specify your desired start date when you apply; typically a few days' advance notice is all you need.

### Gas（ガス）

**A gas company technician must come to your home to perform the「開栓」(gas activation) procedure, and you or an adult member of your household must be present** — it cannot be done remotely or without someone there. Contact the gas company as early as possible before your move to book an appointment and confirm the time.

Important: **Do not use any gas appliances (water heater, stove, etc.) before the 開栓 work is completed.** The technician will inspect the pipe connections and confirm everything is safe before turning on the gas.

### Water（水道）

In most rental situations, water is handled by the landlord or property management company, so you don't need to do anything — it should be ready to use from day one. If you do need to apply yourself, simply contact the 水道局 (water authority) for your area. Check with your landlord or real estate agent beforehand to confirm whether you need to set it up independently.

---

## How to Apply

**Before moving in**, contact each provider to schedule your service start date. Your lease documents or the information packet from your landlord usually include contact details for the local electric and gas companies — be sure to look these up in advance.

When contacting providers, you'll typically need to provide:

- Your full new address (including apartment/unit number)
- Your planned move-in date (i.e., when you want service to start)
- Your name (in kanji or kana)
- A contact phone number

**When moving out**, you'll also need to contact each provider to cancel your service and give your move-out date. If you don't do this proactively, charges will keep accumulating.

## Notes for Foreign Residents

### Language Support

Phone customer service in Japan **is usually only available in Japanese**, which can make calling difficult if you're not fluent. It's generally easier to use the **online application forms** on each company's official website — some even offer multilingual interfaces, making it even simpler.

If you prefer to visit an in-person counter, some companies in major cities offer foreign language assistance — commonly in English, Chinese, and Korean; other languages are often handled via tablet translation apps. Language service availability varies by region and company, so it's worth checking in advance.

### NHK Subscription Fee（受信料）

After moving in, an NHK (Japan's national broadcaster) representative may knock on your door asking you to sign a contract and pay the 受信料 (reception fee).

**Important:** If you **don't have a television** (including any device that can receive terrestrial or BS broadcasts), you are **legally not obligated to pay**. If someone comes to your door, simply say「テレビはありません」("I don't have a TV") — you are not required to sign anything. Don't feel pressured.
