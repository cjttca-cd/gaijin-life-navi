# Studying at a Japanese Language School: Choosing Wisely and Preparing for Exams

For many foreign nationals coming to Japan to study, a Japanese language school is the first stop. But choosing the wrong school — or ignoring your attendance rate — can derail your university plans, or even put your visa renewal at risk. This guide walks you through the key factors to consider when choosing a school and preparing for exams, so you can avoid the most common pitfalls.

---

## Attendance Rate: The Visa Risk Nobody Talks About

**An attendance rate below 80% is a red flag for 入管 (Immigration Services Agency).**

When you renew your residence status, 入管 reviews your attendance records. Without a solid explanation, there's a real risk of having your renewal denied — or even being deported. This is the issue most new students underestimate: many have already accumulated too many absences by the end of their first semester.

Think of your attendance rate as the bare minimum required to stay in Japan legally, not as an aspirational goal.

---

## Four Key Factors When Choosing a School

Don't take ads or agency recommendations at face value. Evaluate schools on these four dimensions yourself:

**1. Track record of university placements**
Have graduates successfully enrolled in national or public universities? This is the most direct indicator of academic quality.

**2. Diversity of student nationalities**
If one nationality makes up over 95% of the student body, your actual opportunities to use Japanese — inside and outside the classroom — drop significantly. Students tend to fall back on their native language outside class. A diverse nationality mix is what genuinely builds real-world Japanese ability.

**3. Location**
Schools in the Greater Tokyo area have higher tuition, but offer richer job opportunities, internship resources, and a Japanese-immersive environment. Regional schools are typically ¥100,000–¥150,000 cheaper per year, but offer fewer employment and language practice opportunities. Weigh this against your goals.

**4. Dropout rate**
Some language schools have dropout rates exceeding 30%. Search online for your target school's dropout data, and cross-check with the school registration information from 文部科学省 (MEXT).

## Agency Recommendations ≠ Your Best Option

Schools recommended through study-abroad agencies often come with kickback arrangements — the recommendation may not be aligned with your academic goals.

What to do instead:
- Look up school registration information directly on the 文部科学省 website
- Contact schools directly and request data on where their graduates have gone
- Read honest reviews from the international student community and compare multiple sources

---

## The Hidden Cost in School Dormitories

Some schools offer dormitories, but at prices well above the local market — the school profits from the housing. When you first arrive, you have limited information to compare prices.

**Practical tip**: Stay in the school dorm for the first 3 months to get familiar with the local rental market, then find your own housing externally. This usually saves a significant amount over the course of your studies.

## Do You Actually Need a Language School?

Some people can skip language school entirely and move straight to the next stage:

- **Strong English speakers**: You may be eligible for English-taught university programs without attending a language school first
- **STEM graduate school applicants**: If you already have informal acceptance (内諾) from your target professor, you can usually apply directly without going through a language school
- **Humanities students already at N1 or above**: You can move directly to university entrance exam preparation

If you're unsure where you fall, it's worth evaluating your Japanese level and academic goals before deciding.

---

## EJU Preparation: Three Points Most People Miss

EJU (日本留学試験) is the core gateway to Japanese universities. These three points are most commonly overlooked by test-takers:

**1. The Japanese section is fundamentally a speed-reading test**
The Japanese section requires completing 17 questions (25 items) in 40 minutes — reading speed is everything. Textbook practice alone won't cut it. Read Japanese books (not textbooks) extensively and listen to Japanese news daily to build real exam speed.

**2. The Comprehensive subject requires current events knowledge**
The Comprehensive subject frequently includes questions on recent Japanese social issues. Students who only review history and geography without following current events risk losing a large number of points in this section.

**3. Government scholarship — an opportunity most students miss**
Students with strong EJU results can apply for a government-funded scholarship of approximately ¥48,000 per month (check the latest official announcements for the current amount). Many international students are completely unaware of this and miss out — it's worth actively looking into.

Also worth noting: some universities only accept EJU scores from either the June or November sitting, not both. Make sure to check each target school's 募集要項 (application guidelines) before registering.
