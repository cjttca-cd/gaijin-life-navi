# Renting in Japan: What Every Foreigner Needs to Know

Renting in Japan as a foreigner is harder than most people expect. Beyond the language barrier, there are plenty of systemic hurdles to clear. Knowing the rules upfront can save you a lot of time and unnecessary expense.

---

## 1. The Reality of Renting as a Foreigner

Japan's rental market isn't exactly welcoming to foreign tenants. Around **40–50% of listings will turn down foreign applicants** — even if you have a long-term visa, steady employment, or a high JLPT score. A common scenario: the 不動産 agent calls the landlord, mentions you're a foreigner, and gets turned down on the spot — sometimes before you even get a chance to view the property (内見).

**How to handle it:**
- Work with multiple 不動産 agents at the same time to maximize your options
- Have your 在留カード, proof of employment, and income documentation ready to submit quickly
- If rejected, ask your agent to negotiate with the landlord — it sometimes works

## 2. 保証会社 and Guarantors

Most rentals in Japan require you to pass a screening through a **保証会社** (guarantor company). Even if a listing says "no guarantor required," foreign tenants are sometimes asked for additional assurances. The screening checks your Japanese credit history — and if you've just arrived in Japan, that history is essentially zero, making approval harder to get.

Key points:
- Being turned down by one 保証会社 doesn't mean game over. Ask your agent whether you can apply through a different 保証会社 instead
- Payment records like mobile phone contracts can help you start building a credit history

## 3. UR賃貸: One of the Easiest Options

**UR賃貸** (rental housing managed by the Urban Renaissance Agency) is worth considering as a top priority for foreign residents:

- 🚫 No guarantor required
- 🚫 No 礼金 (key money)
- 🚫 No 仲介手数料 (agency fee)
- 🚫 No 更新料 (renewal fee)
- ✅ Well-established track record of accepting foreign tenants — rarely rejected for nationality

Eligibility: monthly income must be at least **4× the monthly rent**, or savings equivalent to **100 months' rent** or more (check the official website for the latest requirements).

The properties tend to be older, though renovated units are becoming more common. Move-out notice is just **15 days** — much more flexible than the 1–2 months typical in standard leases.

---

## 4. Move-In Costs: Some Things Are Negotiable

The upfront costs in Japan (敷金, 礼金, 仲介手数料, and more) can add up quickly, but there's often more room to negotiate than people realize:

- **Properties that have been empty for a while:** Try asking for 礼金 to be waived or reduced
- **フリーレント (free rent period):** Negotiate this as part of agreeing to a longer lease
- **Avoid peak season:** January through March is peak rental season in Japan — competition is fierce and discounts are rare
- **Check every add-on fee:** 鍵交換費 (lock replacement), disinfection fees, 24-hour security services — some of these are optional. Always ask: "Is this required?"

---

## 5. Moving In and Moving Out

**When you move in:**
- Take dated photos of every existing scratch, stain, or damage in the unit — this is your most important evidence when you eventually move out

**When you move out:**
- The Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism's 原状回復ガイドライン makes it clear: **wear and tear from normal living (e.g., sun-faded walls, furniture indentations) is the landlord's responsibility** — you're only liable for damage caused by your own negligence or intentional acts
- If you disagree with any charges at the move-out inspection, **don't sign anything on the spot** — say "I'll get back to you after reviewing this"
- Unfair charges can be reported to the 消費生活センター (☎ **188**) — there are real cases where charges have been reversed

---

## 6. Hidden Clauses in the Contract

- **特約 (special clauses):** If the contract's special clauses explicitly state that ハウスクリーニング費 (cleaning fee) is the tenant's responsibility, it may legally hold up. Read every clause carefully before signing
- **火災保険 (fire insurance):** Your landlord may specify a particular insurance provider, but it's worth asking if you can choose your own. Independent policies are usually cheaper
