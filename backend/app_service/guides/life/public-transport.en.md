# Complete Guide to IC Cards & 定期券 — Getting Around Japan by Public Transport

One of the first things newcomers notice about Japan is how extensive and reliable the train and bus network is. To make the most of it, an **IC card** is the one essential item you'll need. IC cards work on trains and buses, and you can also use them at convenience stores, vending machines, and many restaurants — making them one of the most-used items in everyday life in Japan.

---

## Types of IC Cards

Different regions of Japan issue their own IC cards, but most are now **mutually compatible** across the country's transit networks and shops:

| Card | Main Region |
|------|-------------|
| Suica | East Japan (Tokyo, Tohoku, Niigata, etc.) |
| PASMO | Greater Tokyo (private railways & subways) |
| ICOCA | Kansai (Osaka, Kyoto, Kobe, etc.) |

> A Suica bought in Tokyo will generally work fine in Osaka and other regions too.  
> You only need one card — just pick up whichever is most common in your area.

---

## How to Buy an IC Card

**At a station ticket machine (the most common way):**

1. Select「IC カード購入」on the main screen (many machines offer English or other language options)
2. Pay the **deposit (デポジット) of ¥500**, then choose an initial top-up amount (usually ¥1,000 minimum)
3. Take your card and confirm that the balance has been loaded correctly

**Returning your card (when leaving Japan or no longer using it):**  
Visit a station service window to get back the ¥500 deposit plus any remaining balance. You'll typically need to show a valid ID.

## How to Top Up

You can add money to your IC card in several ways:

- **Station ticket machines**: Cash or credit card, usually in increments of ¥1,000–¥10,000
- **Convenience store registers**: Most コンビニ accept cash top-ups — just tell the cashier
- **Smartphone apps** (Mobile Suica / Mobile PASMO): Link a credit or bank card and top up anytime from your phone

The maximum balance is **¥20,000** (subject to each operator's current rules). Always check your balance before boarding — if it's too low, the ticket gate will reject you and you'll need to top up before going through.

## 定期券 (Commuter/Student Pass)

If you travel the same route every day for work or school, a **定期券** can save you a significant amount of money over time. The pass is loaded directly onto your IC card, so there's no paper ticket to carry around.

**How to get one:**

1. Confirm your start and end stations
2. Go to a station service window or a ticket machine that supports 定期券, and select「定期券購入」
3. Enter your route, validity period (1 month / 3 months / 6 months), and personal details
4. Complete payment — the 定期券 is written to your IC card immediately

> A **3- or 6-month 定期券** usually offers a better discount than buying month by month. If your route is fixed, the longer option is worth considering.  
> If your employer or school covers your commuting costs, keep your receipt for reimbursement.

## Common Things to Watch Out For

- **Insufficient balance**: The gate won't let you through if your balance is too low. Top up at a nearby machine before trying again
- **Forgetting to tap out**: If you tap in but forget to tap out, the gate will error next time you enter. Head to the station staff room (改札口 window) and explain — staff can sort it out manually
- **Lost IC card**: If your card isn't registered to your name, any remaining balance is usually gone for good. Consider using the app version, or register your card in advance so a replacement can be issued if it's lost
- **Incompatible lines**: A small number of local private railways or trams may not accept national IC cards. Check the signs at the station before you board

---

## Getting Help

Major stations in large cities usually have ticket machines with multilingual displays. If you run into trouble, **heading directly to the staff room beside the ticket gates is far more effective than calling** — staff there can assist in person, and many stations have multilingual support.
