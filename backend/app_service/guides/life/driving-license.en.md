# Converting Your Foreign Driver's License in Japan (外免切替)

If you're planning to drive in Japan long-term, you'll need to convert your home country license to a Japanese one. This process is called **外免切替 (がいめんきりかえ, gaimen kirikae)**. You don't need to start from scratch with driving school — but the process has a few tricky spots, especially since the **October 2025 reform** made the written test significantly harder. Plenty of people show up unprepared and end up wasting a trip.

---

## Step 1: Figure Out Which Category You're In

Depending on where your license was issued, the procedure falls into one of three categories:

**① Fully Exempt (about 30 countries)**
Both the written and practical tests are waived. Once your documents are reviewed and approved, you'll receive a Japanese license directly. This mainly applies to certain countries in Europe, North America, and Oceania.

**② Partial Exemption (France, Germany, Belgium, Monaco, Switzerland, Taiwan, etc.)**
Holders of licenses from these countries/regions can drive legally in Japan for up to 1 year using their foreign license plus an official Japanese translation from a designated agency. After that year, you'll still need to complete the full conversion process.

**③ Written Test + Practical Test (China, most U.S. states, etc.)**
This is the most involved route — you'll need to pass both a written exam and a practical driving test. Most residents from Asian countries fall into this category.

## ⚠️ Important: October 2025 Reform

Starting October 2025, the number of questions on the 外免切替 written test jumped from **10 to 50**, with a passing score of **90% (45 correct answers)**. After the reform, the pass rate dropped sharply from over 90% down to around **33%**.

The content has also expanded from basic traffic signs to include **traffic regulations and accident liability** — a level of difficulty close to Japan's own 仮免 (provisional license) written exam. If you've heard that "the 外免切替 written test is a breeze" — that era ended in October 2025. Prepare seriously.

---

## Required Documents

- Your original home country driver's license (expired licenses are not accepted)
- Official Japanese translation of your license (must be requested in advance from a designated agency — same-day processing is generally not available)
- Passport (with all entry/exit stamps)
- 在留カード (Residence Card)
- 住民票 (Certificate of Residence)

**Watch out: passport stamp issues**
If you used automated eGates at the border, your passport may not have a manual entry stamp — which makes it difficult to prove you resided in your home country for at least 3 consecutive months. If this applies to you, you'll need to request an immigration record certificate (出入国記録証明) from the 入管局, which takes time. Check this early.

The languages available for the written test vary by exam site and prefecture — contact your local 運転免許センター in advance to confirm.

---

## Practical Test: It's All About Following the Japanese Procedure

The practical test isn't really about how well you can drive — it's about whether you follow **Japan's specific step-by-step procedures** correctly:

- Before getting in the car, walk all the way around it and check underneath (scoring starts here)
- Start-up sequence: adjust mirrors → buckle seatbelt → press brake → start engine → check mirrors → signal → visual check → move. Skipping steps means losing points
- **Left turns require a visual check to the left rear** (巻き込み確認 — checking for cyclists or pedestrians). Missing this is a major deduction
- In the S-curve or right-angle course, if you go off course, **you're allowed to reverse and retry**; forcing through without stopping results in immediate disqualification
- Each exam site has its own course and evaluation tendencies — learning your specific site's quirks in advance can genuinely improve your chances

## Booking and Wait Times

Availability varies a lot between 運転免許センター locations. At busy sites, you may be waiting **several weeks to over a month** for an appointment. Once you've confirmed your documents, book as early as possible — don't wait until your license or residence status is about to expire.
