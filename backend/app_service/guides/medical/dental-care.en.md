# Dental Care in Japan — What Foreigners Need to Know

Visiting the dentist in Japan can feel quite different from what you're used to back home. Multiple appointments, the line between insurance-covered and out-of-pocket treatments, and the real level of "foreign-language service" at clinics — these are all common points of confusion for foreigners going to a Japanese dentist for the first time. This guide draws on real experiences from foreigners living in Japan to help you know what to expect and avoid unnecessary surprises.

---

## Insurance Coverage: Broader Than You Might Think

Japan's health insurance covers a surprisingly wide range of dental treatments. The following are generally covered:

- **Regular cleaning (teeth scaling / tartar removal)**
- **Wisdom tooth extractions**
- **Root canal treatment**
- **White fillings for small cavities**

Note that insurance-covered fillings default to silver amalgam. White fillings are covered when the cavity is small, but typically become self-pay for larger areas.

Before treatment begins, it's worth asking your dentist to confirm whether the procedure falls within insurance coverage — this helps you avoid unexpected costs afterward.

## Watch Out for "Self-Pay Steering"

In areas with a high concentration of foreigners, some clinics that advertise "foreign-language service" have been known to steer patients toward self-pay treatments for procedures that could otherwise be covered by insurance. This has been reported multiple times by foreigners living in Japan, and it's not limited to dental clinics — general practitioners can do this too.

**Common tactics include:**
- Asking you to sign a self-pay consent form at your first visit
- Explaining that a treatment needs to be handled outside insurance because a "specialist" will be involved

**What to do:** At your first visit, proactively say 「保険診療の範囲でお願いします」(Please treat me within the scope of my insurance). Stating this upfront can help cut down on unnecessary out-of-pocket charges.

## Multiple Visits Are the Norm in Japan

A typical Japanese dental appointment lasts 15–30 minutes, and treatment is often spread across multiple sessions. Something that might be handled in a single visit in many countries can easily take 3–5 appointments in Japan — this is partly tied to how insurance billing is structured here.

Knowing this upfront will help you plan your schedule accordingly.

**Tip:** At your first visit, you can tell the dentist 「通院回数を最小限にしたい」(I'd like to minimize the number of visits). Some dentists will try to accommodate this where possible.

## The Reality of "Foreign-Language" Dental Clinics

Clinics that advertise "English-friendly" service often only have front desk staff with English ability — the dentist themselves will typically still conduct the exam and treatment in Japanese.

In practice, many foreigners get by just fine at regular Japanese-speaking clinics with the help of a translation app. When searching for a clinic that suits your needs, word-of-mouth recommendations from the foreign community (such as expat social media groups or online forums) tend to be one of the most reliable sources of information.
