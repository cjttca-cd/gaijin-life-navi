# Seeking Medical Care in Japan: Common Traps for Foreigners and How to Handle Them

There are many hidden barriers for foreigners seeking medical care in Japan. Knowing about them in advance can save you a lot of trouble and unnecessary expenses.

---

## Being Refused Treatment on Language Grounds

Although Article 19 of Japan's 医師法 (Medical Practitioners Act) stipulates that medical institutions have an obligation to accept patients, clinics do in practice sometimes refuse foreign patients citing difficulty communicating in their language — this is especially common in rural areas and at 産婦人科 (obstetrics and gynecology) clinics.

**How to handle it**:
- Don't give up if you're refused. Contact AMDA 国際医療情報センター (03-6233-9266) and ask them to refer you to a medical institution that accepts foreign patients.
- Large hospitals generally have better foreign-language support and can be considered as an alternative.

## The Cost of Going Directly to a Large Hospital Without a Referral Letter

Many foreigners assume that "big hospitals are better for English" and head there directly, but in Japan, first-time patients at large hospitals without a 紹介状 (referral letter) are required to pay an additional 選定療養費 (special facility fee). This portion is not covered by insurance and must be paid entirely out of pocket.

**Recommended process**: First visit a regular クリニック (clinic) → have the clinic issue a 紹介状 → then go to a large hospital.

## The Risks of Seeking Medical Care Without Insurance

Those holding a 在留資格 (residence status) in Japan are obligated to enroll in 国民健康保険（国保）. If you seek medical care without having enrolled in 国保, some medical institutions may charge you under 自由診療 (which is typically 2–3 times the insured rate).

In addition, when paying back premiums for 国保, they are backdated to cover the period you were uninsured, so it is advisable to complete enrollment as early as possible.

## Payment Tips When Visiting a Clinic

- Most small clinics do not accept credit cards — **be sure to bring cash**
- 問診票 (medical questionnaires) are usually only available in Japanese; large hospitals occasionally have English versions, but small clinics generally do not
- **Prepare in advance**: Write a simple summary in Japanese of your symptoms, medical history, allergies, and current medications

## About JMIP-Certified Multilingual Hospitals

JMIP certification means a hospital has a multilingual service system in place, but it does not guarantee that every doctor can communicate in a foreign language. These hospitals are mostly university-level hospitals and are best suited as a choice for **specialist treatment or hospitalization** rather than as a first choice for everyday medical visits.
