# Pregnancy & Childbirth Guide for Foreign Residents in Japan

Pregnancy and childbirth in Japan can be an exciting yet challenging experience for foreign residents. Between navigating language barriers and unfamiliar systems, it helps to know the key steps and procedures ahead of time — so you're not caught off guard when it matters most.

---

## 1. Pregnancy Registration & 母子手帳

After confirming your pregnancy, **your first step is to visit your local 役所 (municipal office)** and submit a 「妊娠届出」 (pregnancy notification). You'll receive two important items:

- **母子手帳 (Mother and Child Health Handbook)**: An official booklet recording maternal and infant health information, used from pregnancy through child-rearing
- **妊婦健診受診票 (prenatal checkup subsidy coupons)**: Covers costs for up to 14 prenatal checkups

⚠️ **Without registering at the 役所, you won't receive the coupons — and all prenatal checkups will be fully out-of-pocket.** Please complete this as soon as possible after confirming your pregnancy.

Note that the coupons have a **maximum reimbursement amount per visit** — any costs above that limit are paid on the spot. When you factor in ultrasounds, special blood tests, amniocentesis, and other exams, most expectant mothers end up paying around **¥70,000–¥100,000** out of pocket over the full pregnancy. The idea that "all 14 checkups are free" is a common misconception — budget accordingly.

Many local governments offer the 母子手帳 in **multiple languages** (English, Chinese, Korean, and more). Ask at the counter when you pick it up. Your 自治体 may also offer extra support like diapers or taxi vouchers — worth asking about so you don't miss out.

## 2. Choosing a Birthing Facility

**Start looking for a facility early.** Popular clinics and hospitals often fill up in the first trimester, so don't wait — reach out as soon as you confirm your pregnancy.

Here are four key things to check:

**① Language support**
Can they communicate with you in your language — through staff or interpretation services? Language barriers during labor can be critical, so this is worth prioritizing.

**② Epidural availability**
Epidural anesthesia is not standard in Japan; natural birth is the cultural norm. Many facilities only offer epidurals on weekdays during daytime hours ("planned birth" model), and may not be able to accommodate requests at night or on weekends when an anesthesiologist isn't available. According to the Japan Society of Obstetrics and Gynecology, more than half of epidurals are not administered by anesthesia specialists. Be sure to ask specifically about the facility's setup and available hours.

**③ Admission and visitation rules**
Policies vary widely — visiting hours, length of stay (typically 5–6 days for vaginal delivery), and rooming-in arrangements. Confirm these in advance.

**④ Episiotomy policy**
Some facilities in Japan still perform episiotomies routinely. The WHO and the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists recommend against routine episiotomy. Ask the facility about their policy before admission.

## 3. 出産育児一時金 (Lump-Sum Birth Allowance)

When you give birth in Japan, you can apply for the **出産育児一時金** (lump-sum birth allowance) through your health insurance. Most hospitals and clinics are enrolled in the **直接支払制度 (direct payment system)**, where the payment goes directly to the facility — so you only pay the difference at discharge, rather than covering the full cost upfront.

⚠️ In urban areas, private maternity hospitals often charge more than the allowance covers. Ask the facility for a cost estimate in advance.

---

## 4. Postpartum Support

**Postpartum care plan (officially implemented in 2024):** At your 2-week and 1-month postpartum checkups, you'll be screened using the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) — this applies to foreign mothers too. The scale is available in **37 languages** and can be downloaded free from the Japan Midwives Association website (midwife.or.jp). You can use it as a self-assessment tool in advance; a score of 9 or above is considered high risk.

**Postpartum care facility costs vary significantly:**

- Public facilities (subsidized by local government): approx. **¥5,000/night** (free for low-income households)
- Private facilities: approx. **¥50,000/night**

⚠️ **A note on postnatal confinement:** Japanese maternity facilities generally don't follow traditional postpartum confinement customs. Meals can vary and may be served cold or at room temperature. If you come from a background where postpartum confinement is important (e.g., Chinese or Korean tradition), check the facility's meal and care arrangements before admission.
