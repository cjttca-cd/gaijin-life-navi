# Traffic Accident Guide for Foreign Residents in Japan

Being involved in a traffic accident in Japan can be especially tough for foreign residents — language barriers and unfamiliarity with the system can easily put you at a disadvantage. From the very first words at the scene to negotiating with insurance companies, every step can affect your final compensation. Knowing what to do ahead of time goes a long way toward protecting your rights when it matters most.

---

## What to Do Immediately After an Accident

**Don't say "すみません (sorry)"**: In Japan, a reflexive apology can be used by the other party as an admission of fault, which directly affects how liability is split. Even if you're rattled, hold back that impulse.

**Don't settle on the spot**: The full extent of damage — especially physical injuries — sometimes doesn't show up until later. On-the-spot settlements are very hard to undo, so don't sign anything or make any promises at the scene.

**Call the police and get the 交通事故証明書**: Even for minor accidents, it's strongly advisable to call the police. The 交通事故証明書 (accident report certificate) is an essential document for any insurance claim or legal process that follows — without it, things get very difficult.

**Gather evidence**: Photograph the scene, vehicle damage, and the positions of the vehicles. If you have a ドライブレコーダー (dashcam), make sure to save the footage — it can be decisive when determining fault. Without one, it's your word against theirs.

**Emergency Japanese cheat sheet (save this to your phone)**:
「警察を呼んでください」(Please call the police) ／ 「救急車をお願いします」(Please call an ambulance) ／ 「サインはできません」(I cannot sign)

## Key Points When Dealing with Insurance Companies

Insurance companies are in the business of minimizing payouts. Even if you're the victim, the first offer on the table is rarely the best one.

A few things to keep in mind:

- **Ask for rental car and taxi costs proactively**: These expenses are usually not offered upfront — you need to ask for them explicitly
- **Don't agree to end treatment prematurely**: Insurance companies may suggest wrapping up medical treatment before you've fully recovered — it's a common tactic to reduce payouts. Don't sign any settlement agreement until you're confident you've fully healed
- **Keep up with your medical visits**: The number of visits to the clinic forms the basis for calculating 慰謝料 (compensation for pain and suffering). If you're not feeling well, go see a doctor — skipping appointments because you're "too busy" is one of the costliest mistakes you can make
- **Prioritize orthopedic clinics (整形外科)**: Treatment received only at a 整骨院 (judo therapy clinic) may not be fully recognized by insurance companies as valid medical records

## Special Considerations for Foreign Residents

### 過失割合 (Fault Ratio)

In Japan, it's rare for an accident to be ruled as 100% the other party's fault (10:0). Even the injured party is usually assigned some degree of liability. If your Japanese isn't fluent, this part of the process can put you at a disadvantage — get professional help early.

### 弁護士費用特約 (Attorney Fee Coverage)

If your car insurance includes **弁護士費用特約** (attorney fee rider), the insurance company covers your 弁護士 (attorney) fees, meaning you can hire a lawyer to handle negotiations at no cost to you. Many people don't even realize they have this coverage, or assume it's only for serious accidents.

Important things to know:
- It can apply to property damage cases too — not just personal injury
- You get to choose your own 弁護士; you're not required to use one the insurer recommends
- Start the process as early as possible — once settlement negotiations have begun, it may be too late to bring in a lawyer

### Impact on Residency Status

- **Drunk driving or serious negligence** can result in criminal charges, which may affect your visa renewal
- Check that your **international driving license is still valid**: driving with an expired international license in Japan counts as driving without a license
- If you're hospitalized for an extended period and unable to work, your employment visa renewal could be affected — talk to your employer early and prepare the necessary documentation

### Bicycle Accidents

In Japan, bicycles are legally classified as vehicles, not pedestrians. If a cyclist collides with a pedestrian on a sidewalk, the cyclist typically bears the greater share of liability. Even in a minor collision, it's advisable to call the police — otherwise you may lack evidence if a dispute arises later.
