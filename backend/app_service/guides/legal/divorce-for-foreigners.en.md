# Guide to Divorce in Japan for Foreign Nationals

Divorce in Japan as a foreign national is often more complicated than it first appears. The Japanese procedures themselves aren't that difficult, but **completing a divorce in Japan does not automatically mean your home country recognizes it**. Overlooking this can lead to a situation where you are legally divorced in Japan but still considered married back home. This guide walks you through the key processes and things to watch out for.

---

## The Three Types of Divorce in Japan

Japanese law recognizes three main forms of divorce:

### 1. 協議離婚 (Consensual Divorce)
If both parties agree voluntarily, you simply submit a 離婚届 (divorce notification form) to the local municipal office (役所) — no court involvement required. This is by far the most common type of divorce in Japan and is relatively straightforward.

**Important:** Don't sign the 離婚届 until you're absolutely sure about the divorce. Once signed, if your spouse submits the form before you, it becomes very difficult to withdraw. If you're worried your spouse might file unilaterally without your consent, you can preemptively submit a **"離婚届不受理申出"** (rikon-todoke fuju-ri moushide) to the municipal office — this is an official system that prevents the form from being accepted without your involvement.

### 2. 調停離婚 (Mediated Divorce)
When the two parties cannot reach an agreement on their own, you must apply for mediation (調停) at the 家庭裁判所 (Family Court). A mediator helps facilitate the process, and the divorce is finalized once an agreement is reached. **Under Japanese law, you must go through mediation before filing a contested divorce lawsuit** — this is a legal requirement and cannot be skipped.

### 3. 裁判離婚 (Judicial Divorce)
If mediation fails, you can file a divorce lawsuit in court. Note that Japanese family courts tend to be conservative about granting divorces on the grounds of mere "incompatibility." You generally need to establish one of the legally recognized grounds for divorce (such as adultery or willful abandonment) to have a strong case.

## International Divorce: Japan's Divorce Doesn't Automatically Count Elsewhere

This is the most commonly overlooked issue for foreign nationals divorcing in Japan. **A divorce completed in Japan may not automatically be valid in your home country.** Some countries require their own court proceedings to recognize a foreign divorce — a Japanese 離婚届 alone may not have legal effect back home. If you don't complete the necessary procedures in your home country, you could end up legally divorced in Japan but still married there, which can affect remarriage, inheritance, and more.

To use Japanese divorce documentation in your home country, you'll typically need:
- **離婚届受理証明書** (a certificate of acceptance of the divorce notification, issued by the 役所)
- **アポスティーユ certification** (an international authentication of official documents by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, recognized in treaty member countries)

The exact requirements vary significantly by nationality. It's strongly recommended to check with your country's embassy or consulate before finalizing the divorce, or consult a 弁護士 (lawyer) experienced in international divorce.

## Impact on Your Residence Status

Foreign nationals holding a **配偶者ビザ** (spousal residence status) need to pay special attention after divorce:

- You will generally need to **change your residence status within 6 months of the divorce**, or risk having it revoked
- Depending on your circumstances, you may be able to apply for a different status such as "定住者" (Long-Term Resident)
- **Permanent residency (永住) is not affected by divorce** — if you hold permanent residency, you don't need to worry about this

If you are divorcing due to domestic violence (DV), there are special protections regarding residence status. It's recommended to consult a specialized support organization as early as possible.

## Property Division, Child Support, and Custody

**年金分割 (Pension Splitting)**
Japan has a system for dividing the 厚生年金 (Employees' Pension) accumulated during the marriage. This must be completed **within 2 years of the divorce**. Coming back to Japan from abroad to handle this later is extremely difficult in practice — take care of it while you're still in Japan.

**Child Support**
Without a written agreement, enforcing child support after just submitting a 離婚届 is very difficult. It's strongly recommended to formally document the amount and payment method through a **公正証書** (kōsei shōsho, a notarized document), which gives it legal enforceability.

**Child Custody (親権)**
In Japan, 親権 (custody) typically goes to the parent who lives with the child. If the child has been living in Japan, it is relatively common for the Japanese spouse to be awarded custody. 面会交流 (visitation rights) are not automatically guaranteed — it's advisable to clearly establish visitation arrangements in the divorce agreement or during the 調停 process.
