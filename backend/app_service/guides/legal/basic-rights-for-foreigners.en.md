# Basic Rights Guide for Foreign Residents in Japan

"Do foreigners have no legal protections in Japan?" — This is a common misconception. In reality, Japan's Constitution and various laws provide quite broad rights protections for foreign residents. Knowing your rights is the first step to protecting yourself. This guide covers seven key areas of basic rights to give you the full picture; for more detail on each area, check the relevant specialized guides or ask the AI Navigator.

---

## 1. Constitutional Rights

According to Japanese Supreme Court precedents (most notably the 1978 マクリーン事件 case), the fundamental human rights provisions in Japan's Constitution apply in nature to foreign residents in Japan. Key rights include:

- **Personal freedom**: You cannot be detained or arrested without cause
- **Right to trial**: You have the right to a fair trial, to retain a lawyer, and to receive interpreter assistance
- **Freedom of expression and religion**: Protected under the Constitution
- **Equality before the law**: You cannot be treated differently based on nationality

*Note: Certain rights inherent to Japanese nationals — such as the right to vote and hold certain public offices — have limited application and are not included here.*

## 2. Social Security Rights

**国民健康保険 (National Health Insurance)**  
Foreign nationals legally residing in Japan (generally with a residence period exceeding 3 months) are required to enroll in 国民健康保険. Once enrolled, a significant portion of your medical costs will be covered by insurance, greatly reducing out-of-pocket expenses.

**国民年金 (National Pension)**  
Foreign residents in Japan between the ages of 20 and 59 are also required to participate in the 国民年金 system. When you leave Japan, you can apply for the 脱退一時金 (lump-sum withdrawal payment) within the specified period after departure.

**生活保護 (Public Assistance)**  
This is principally for Japanese nationals, but foreign residents holding certain statuses such as permanent residence (永住), long-term residence (定住), or spouse of a Japanese national are generally provided equivalent assistance by municipalities on humanitarian grounds.

## 3. Children's Right to Education

Children of foreign residents in Japan **have the right to attend public elementary and junior high schools**. Municipalities are obligated to send school enrollment notices to foreign families and provide guidance and support.

**Even if a child's residence status is uncertain**, schools and municipalities **cannot refuse enrollment on the grounds of residence status** — this is a clear guideline from the Ministry of Education, Culture, Sports, Science and Technology. Many families keep their children out of school out of concern about residence status, but there is actually no legal basis for such concern.

## 4. Labor Rights

Japan's Labor Standards Act and minimum wage regulations **apply to all workers in Japan regardless of nationality**:

- **Minimum wage guarantee**: Employers cannot pay below the legally mandated minimum wage for any reason
- **Protection against unfair dismissal**: Dismissal is restricted even during the probationary period. If you are pressured to quit, verbally agreeing on the spot does not constitute an official resignation — signing anything on the spot is extremely risky
- **Right to overtime pay**: Work beyond statutory hours must be compensated in accordance with the law
- **Right to join a union**: Individuals can join a community union (合同組合) and use collective bargaining to bring employers to the negotiating table

Labor disputes involve nuances around evidence preservation, complaint channels, and impact on residence status that vary case by case. We recommend consulting the dedicated labor disputes guide.

## 5. Housing Protections

In the rental housing sector, the law places certain constraints on discrimination based on nationality (including best-effort obligation provisions under the 住宅セーフティネット法 and related regulations). If you are refused tenancy on the grounds of nationality, you can raise the issue with a 消費生活相談 (consumer affairs consultation) window or a housing consultation service.

---

## 6. DV and Crime Victim Protections

**Regardless of residence status**, foreign nationals who are victims of domestic violence (DV) or crime are protected by law:

- The 配偶者暴力防止法 (Act on Prevention of Spousal Violence) applies to all residents in Japan, and victims can apply for a protection order (such as a no-contact order)
- Seeking help from a DV support organization **will not lead to your residence status being investigated**
- In police and judicial proceedings, foreign national victims have the right to request interpreter assistance

## 7. Access to Legal Aid

If your rights have been violated, here are ways to access legal support:

- **法テラス (Japan Legal Support Center)**: Provides multilingual information services. If you meet the financial eligibility criteria, you can apply for the attorney fee advance system (repaid in installments afterward; repayment may be waived for those receiving 生活保護)
- **弁護士会 free consultations**: Each prefecture's 弁護士会 (bar association) regularly holds free legal consultations specifically for foreign residents
- **Before your consultation**: Summarize the situation on a single A4 sheet; bring relevant documents (contracts, pay stubs, correspondence records, etc.) and your 在留カード; be clear about "what happened" and "what outcome you want"

*Note: People in irregular residence situations such as overstaying may face restrictions on eligibility for certain civil legal aid. We recommend checking with a consultation window first.*
