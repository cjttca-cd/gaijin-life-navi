# Guide to Labor Rights for Foreign Workers in Japan

Foreign workers in Japan are protected by the same labor laws as Japanese workers — including the 労働基準法. Many people, however, don't know their rights and aren't sure what to do when they're treated unfairly. This guide covers the most common issues foreign workers face and what you can do about them.

---

## 1. Dismissal and Resignation Pressure: Know Your Rights

Japan has strict rules around dismissal. **Even during a probationary period, employers cannot fire workers arbitrarily** — the common belief that "you can be freely dismissed during probation" is a misconception. Workers are still protected by law, and dismissals without just cause can be legally challenged.

If your company suggests you "voluntarily resign" (退職勧奨), keep the following in mind:

- **Don't make any commitments on the spot**: Even saying "I understand" verbally could be interpreted as agreement to resign
- **Don't sign any documents related to dismissal or resignation**: Once you sign, disputing it becomes much harder
- After receiving a dismissal notice, you can express your objection in writing (e.g., by email) — keep a backup copy in your personal inbox

## 2. Preserving Evidence

In labor disputes, evidence is your most important asset:

- Save all **email and chat message** records
- Keep **records of overtime** (daily work logs, time-card records, email timestamps, etc.)
- As soon as you sense a dispute brewing, transfer relevant records to your personal device or email account

---

## 3. Where to Get Help

### 労働基準監督署 (Labor Standards Inspection Office)

This office handles violations of labor law, including unpaid overtime and unlawful dismissal. You can file a complaint (申告), and the office will investigate and instruct the company to correct the issue. Note that the mediation process (あっせん) is **voluntary** — companies can refuse to participate, so it's not a guaranteed resolution mechanism.

### Foreign Worker Consultation Hotline

A multilingual consultation service operated by the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare. For current contact details and available languages, please check the official website.

### 合同労働組合 (Individual-Membership Unions)

Individuals can join a "合同労組." These unions have collective bargaining rights and can **compel companies to negotiate**. If a company refuses to cooperate with the 労働基準監督署, going through a union can be more effective.

---

## 4. Workplace Harassment

Workplace harassment (パワハラ・セクハラ) is regulated by law in Japan. Here's what to know:

- When the harasser is a supervisor, the internal complaint process is often **not very effective in practice** — it's worth keeping external channels as an option
- If you were forced to resign due to harassment, the reason recorded for your resignation directly affects your unemployment insurance eligibility and payout period. **Always check what reason is listed on your 離職票 (separation certificate)**

---

## 5. Technical Intern Trainees: Special Considerations

Technical intern trainees generally cannot change employers freely. If you face problems, you can file a complaint with the **外国人技能実習機構**, which offers consultations in your native language.

---

## 6. Impact on Your Visa Status

Labor issues and residency status are closely linked:

- If you are on a work visa, **you may risk losing your residency status if you don't find a new job within approximately 3 months of leaving your position**
- After resigning or changing jobs, you must submit a change notification to the 出入国在留管理局 **within 14 days**
- Whether your reason for leaving is classified as "自己都合" (personal reasons) or "会社都合" (company reasons) directly affects your unemployment insurance eligibility and benefit period. **Check the details carefully on your 離職票**
