# Workplace Discrimination and Harassment: A Guide for Foreign Residents in Japan

Discrimination and harassment in Japanese workplaces can be especially tricky for foreign residents — sometimes unfair treatment gets dismissed as a "language barrier," and definitions of harassment may differ between your home country and Japan. Knowing your rights and the steps you can take is the first line of self-protection.

---

## Common Situations Foreign Residents Face

Workplace harassment (パワハラ / パワーハラスメント) and discrimination are regulated by law in Japan. Situations that foreign residents tend to encounter include:

- **Disadvantageous transfers justified by "not enough Japanese"**: Being reassigned to roles unrelated to your original position, or facing demotions or workplace isolation without legitimate reason
- **Workplace bullying (パワハラ)**: Psychological pressure beyond the scope of work, unreasonable criticism, or being excluded and ostracized at the workplace
- **Cultural differences in how harassment is defined**: Behavior that wouldn't be considered a problem in your home country may already constitute harassment in Japan — and vice versa. If you're unsure, start by keeping a record

## What to Do After Something Happens

### Step 1: Preserve Evidence Right Away

This is the single most important thing you can do. Save as much of the following as possible:

- Emails, chat logs, and screenshots
- Voice recordings of meetings (recording by the involved party is generally considered acceptable based on community experience, but check with a professional for your specific situation)
- A work journal: log dates, what happened, and who was present

### Step 2: Be Realistic About Internal Complaint Channels

Community experience shows that when the harasser is your direct supervisor, internal complaint procedures rarely deliver meaningful results. If internal channels don't resolve the issue, external organizations are often a stronger option — you don't need to wait until internal procedures are exhausted before seeking outside help.

### Step 3: Pay Close Attention to How Your Reason for Leaving Is Recorded

If you're considering resigning due to harassment or discrimination, **how the reason for resignation is recorded on your 離職票 (separation certificate) has a significant impact on your unemployment benefits**. If it's recognized as "company-initiated" (会社都合), the waiting period before benefits begin and the duration of payments are both more favorable than "voluntary resignation." Before signing any resignation-related documents, consult a professional.

Also, if you hold a 就労ビザ (work visa), leaving your job without finding a new one within 3 months may put your residence status at risk of being revoked. When resigning or changing jobs, you are also required to **notify the 入管 (入国管理局 / Immigration Services Agency) within 14 days**.

## Where to Get Help

### 労働基準監督署 (Labor Standards Inspection Office)

Accepts reports of violations of labor conditions and can facilitate mediation (あっせん). Note that mediation is voluntary — if the company refuses to participate, it cannot be forced. Some offices may not have foreign-language services, so it's worth checking beforehand.

### 法テラス (Japan Legal Support Center)

Provides legal information in multiple languages. Those who qualify financially can apply for legal fee advances (repaid in installments; recipients of public assistance may be exempt in some cases). Note: multilingual services cover "information provision" — for specific legal advice, you'll need a direct consultation with a lawyer.

### 合同労組 (Individual Union / Community Union)

A labor union you can join as an individual. These unions have the right to collective bargaining and can demand that the company attend negotiations. The level of support for foreign workers varies between unions, so check beforehand whether they have experience with issues involving foreign residents.

### Free Legal Consultations at 役所 (Municipal Office)

Many local 役所 offices offer free legal consultations, and you can often request an interpreter. This is a great first step before formally hiring a lawyer — to understand your basic options, get a sense of direction, and find out what kind of professional you should be consulting.

---

## How to Prepare for a Consultation

Thirty minutes goes by fast. Preparing in advance will make a huge difference:

- Summarize the timeline of events on a single A4 sheet of paper
- Bring all relevant documents (employment contract, pay slips, emails, chat screenshots, etc.)
- **Bring your 在留カード (Residence Card)**: lawyers need to understand your residence status — it's important information for assessing your case
- Think in advance about "what happened" and "what outcome you want," and take notes on the lawyer's advice and recommended next steps
- Consider consulting at least 2–3 professionals and make use of free consultation opportunities to compare perspectives

One important note: for disputes involving harassment or discrimination, consult a **弁護士 (licensed attorney)** — not an 行政書士 or 司法書士.
