# Insurance Basics for Foreign Residents in Japan

Japan's insurance system is divided into two main categories: **public insurance** and **private insurance**. Foreign residents are required to join one of the public health insurance programs, and can optionally add private insurance based on their needs. This guide covers the key structures of each type and common points to watch out for as a foreign resident.

---

## Public Health Insurance: Mandatory Enrollment

In Japan, almost all residents (including foreigners) must enroll in one of two health insurance programs:

**社会保険** (Shakai Hoken)
- For: Employees working full-time at a company
- Premiums are split between employer and employee

**国民健康保険** (National Health Insurance / 国保)
- For: Students, self-employed individuals, freelancers, and anyone not covered by 社会保険
- You'll need to visit your local 役所 (municipal office) to enroll

### Premium Reduction (Important: You Must Apply)

Students and low-income residents can apply for a significant reduction in 国保 premiums — but **you must apply for it yourself; it's not automatic**. Those with no income may qualify for near-full exemption. The reduction must be renewed each year, so don't forget to reapply.

### 高額療養費制度 (High-Cost Medical Care System)

Public health insurance caps your monthly out-of-pocket expenses through a system called **高額療養費制度**. Any costs above the cap are covered by insurance. This means your actual costs for hospitalization or serious illness are often much lower than the sticker price — and it's an important factor when deciding whether private medical insurance is worth it for you.

Also, if you're enrolled in 国保 and need medical care while abroad, you can apply for **海外療養費** reimbursement at your 役所. Make sure to keep your receipts and diagnosis documentation.

## Private Insurance: Types and What to Watch Out For

> Note: Under Japanese law, residents in Japan cannot purchase insurance products from overseas insurance companies.

### Life Insurance & Medical Insurance

There are a few things to know when applying for private insurance as a foreign resident:

- **Death coverage (life insurance)**: Some major insurers require that you have a family member (including a spouse) residing in Japan. Even permanent residents (永住者) can be declined — check the insurer's head office policy before applying.
- **Medical insurance & cancer insurance**: These generally don't have the same family member requirement, and foreign residents can usually apply without issue.
- **Nationality restrictions**: A small number of insurers have restrictions based on nationality. It's worth confirming in advance.
- **Language requirements**: Most applications require a Japanese-language phone verification call. Prepare accordingly.

共済 (cooperative insurance) is also available to foreign residents once they join an eligible cooperative. Premiums are typically lower than commercial insurance, and there's often a year-end premium refund.

### 自動車保険 (Car Insurance)

- **自賠責保険** (Compulsory Insurance): Required by law. Covers bodily injury to others only — property damage is not included.
- **任意保険** (Optional Insurance): Strongly recommended. Add property damage coverage and attorney fee riders. Vehicle repair costs in Japan are high, and compulsory insurance alone rarely covers real-world accident costs.

**If you hold a foreign driver's license:**
- An International Driving Permit (IDP) is only valid for 1 year after entry into Japan. Driving past that deadline counts as unlicensed driving, and your insurance may be void in the event of an accident.
- Your accident-free driving record from overseas is not recognized in Japan. You'll start at the lowest rating level, which means higher premiums initially.

### 火災保険・地震保険 (Fire & Earthquake Insurance)

- **火災保険** (Fire Insurance): Real estate agents typically point you toward a specific insurer when renting, but unless your contract explicitly states it's mandatory, you're legally free to choose your own. Coverage usually extends beyond fire — flooding from upstairs neighbors, sudden accidents, and similar events are often included.
- **地震保険** (Earthquake Insurance): Must be purchased as an add-on to fire insurance. Rates are set uniformly nationwide by the government, so there's no need to shop around. Note that payouts are capped and won't fully cover rebuilding costs.

## Childbirth Benefits: Two Programs That Are Easy to Mix Up

Foreign residents in Japan often confuse these two programs:

| Benefit | Who qualifies | What it is |
|---------|--------------|------------|
| 出産育児一時金 | Anyone enrolled in 国保 or 社会保険 | A one-time lump-sum childbirth payment |
| 出産手当金 | Employed women enrolled in 社会保険 only | Income replacement during maternity leave |

Those enrolled in 国保 (students, self-employed, freelancers, etc.) **are not eligible for 出産手当金**. Keep this distinction in mind.

Also, as of 2024, 国保 enrollees can apply for premium reductions during the prenatal and postnatal period. Head to your 役所 for the details.
