# International Money Transfer Guide for Foreign Residents in Japan

Living in Japan, most money transfer needs fall into three categories: **sending money to family abroad**, **receiving funds from overseas**, and **getting emergency cash when you first arrive and don't have a Japanese bank account yet**. This guide covers the types of transfer options available, what to keep in mind for each situation, and what to prepare before you go.

---

## 1. Types of Transfer Options

International money transfers in Japan broadly fall into three categories:

### Type 1: Bank International Transfers

Transfer money through a Japanese bank's international wire service. This is the most formal route and works for most situations. Banks vary in terms of fees, processing time, and how you can initiate transfers — **some banks have discontinued online transfers and require you to visit a branch in person**. You'll typically need your residence card, your マイナンバー (Individual Number), and the recipient's full banking details.

### Type 2: Specialist Remittance Services

Online platforms built specifically for personal international transfers. These typically offer **lower fees, transparent exchange rates, and faster delivery**, supporting a wide range of currencies. You'll generally need your residence card or other ID to open an account, though requirements vary by service.

### Type 3: ATM Cash Withdrawal with an Overseas Card

If you haven't opened a Japanese bank account yet, you can withdraw cash at convenience store ATMs using overseas-issued **Visa, Mastercard, UnionPay, or AMEX** cards. This is **the most practical way to get cash during your first days in Japan**.

---

## 2. Preparing Before You Arrive

If you plan to use your overseas bank card for cash withdrawals after arriving in Japan, **take care of these things before you leave**:

- **Enable overseas withdrawal**: Some cards have international cash access turned off by default — contact your card issuer in advance to enable it
- **Check your withdrawal limits**: Make sure your daily and per-transaction limits are high enough to cover your initial expenses
- **Confirm card compatibility**: Cards that only have an IC chip and no magnetic stripe may not work at some ATMs — check with your card issuer before departing

---

## 3. Getting Cash When You First Arrive

Before you've opened a local bank account, convenience store ATMs are your most reliable source of cash.

- Major convenience store ATMs accept international cards including Visa, Mastercard, UnionPay, and AMEX
- **Convenience store ATM exchange rates are generally better than airport currency exchange counters** — avoid changing large amounts at the airport if you can
- Each withdrawal has a cap, and fees vary depending on your card issuer and card type

---

## 4. Sending Money Abroad

### Documents Typically Required

- **Residence card**
- **マイナンバー (Individual Number)** — international transfers are one of the situations where マイナンバー is required
- Recipient's bank account details (account number, bank name, bank code, etc.)
- Purpose of the transfer

### Notes on Branch Counter Transfers

Some banks only allow international transfers at a physical branch. Counter staff will typically ask about the purpose of the transfer and your relationship to the recipient, so have that information ready.

> **Tip:** Before sending, confirm the receiving requirements with your recipient's bank to avoid delays or returned transfers due to mismatched information.

---

## 5. Receiving Money from Abroad

When receiving an international transfer into your Japanese bank account, keep these points in mind:

- **Arrival times vary by bank**: Some banks process international transfers through correspondent banks (intermediary banks), which can add extra time
- **Additional documents may be required**: For larger amounts, some banks may ask for your マイナンバー or other supporting documents
- **Check in advance**: Confirming requirements with your receiving bank in Japan before the money is sent can help prevent delays
